# Summary: Shipping, Supply Chain & Negative Impacts

## Abstract

An Iran-US/Israel military conflict creates a bifurcated trade landscape within the shipping and travel sectors: tanker/shipping stocks are major beneficiaries of Strait of Hormuz disruption, while airlines, cruise lines, online travel agencies, and oil-import-dependent emerging markets face severe downside. VLCC spot rates have already surged to record February levels ($110,000-$170,000/day) ahead of the February 28 strikes, with Frontline (FRO) booking 92% of 2026 VLCC days at $107,100/day and Scorpio Tankers (STNG) operating at historically low $11,000/day breakeven costs. War risk insurance premiums have spiked 60% for Gulf-transiting vessels (from 0.125% to 0.2-0.7% of hull value), directly benefiting tanker owners through surcharges passed to charterers. On the short side, airlines face a triple threat of airspace closures (8 Middle Eastern states shut airspace on Feb 28), $6,000+/hour rerouting costs, and jet fuel spikes from oil at $80-150+/bbl. Cruise lines (RCL, CCL, NCLH) face fuel cost pressure and consumer confidence erosion, while emerging markets like India (85% oil import dependent) and Turkey (Iran border instability) face capital flight to safe havens. The Red Sea Houthi crisis (2023-25) provides the closest modern precedent, where tanker rates surged 132% YoY and container rates rose sevenfold, but a Hormuz closure would be an order of magnitude more disruptive given the strait handles 20% of global oil consumption versus the Red Sea's ~12% of container traffic.

## Key Insights

### Tanker Stocks — Major Beneficiaries

- **VLCC rates at record February levels**: The Baltic Exchange VLCC day rate averaged $110,854 in February 2026 — the best February on record — with peak rates reaching $170,000/day, tripling since January 2026. Geopolitical risk premium and strong Chinese demand are the primary drivers. [Source: splash247.com, maritime-hub.com, energyplanets.org]

- **War risk insurance surges 60%+**: Premiums for Gulf-transiting vessels jumped from ~0.125% to 0.2-0.4% of hull and machinery (H&M) value, with Israel/US-affiliated vessels seeing quotes as high as 0.7%. VLCC spot rates on ME-China route surged 154% in the week following military intervention. These costs are passed through to charterers as surcharges, effectively boosting tanker owner revenues. [Source: insurancebusinessmag.com, spglobal.com, bertling.com]

- **FRO (Frontline) — Best-positioned VLCC pure play**: Fleet of 41 VLCCs, 21 Suezmax, 18 LR2 tankers (average age 7.5 years, 100% eco, 57% scrubber-fitted). Q4 2025 adjusted profit $230.4M ($1.03/share), revenue $624.5M. Cash breakeven at $24,300/day average ($25,000 VLCC). Already booked 92% of 2026 VLCC days at $107,100/day. Cash generation potential: $2.8B ($12.51/share), implying 34% yield at recent prices. Acquired 9 newbuild VLCCs for $1B while selling 8 older vessels for $831.5M — fleet renewal positions for sustained high-rate environment. [Source: globenewswire.com, fool.com, investing.com]

- **STNG (Scorpio Tankers) — Product tanker leader with fortress balance sheet**: Fleet of 99 vessels (38 LR2, 47 MR, 14 Handymax). Q4 2025 net income $128.1M, full year $344.3M. Cash breakeven at industry-low $11,000/day. Cash position $937.1M with $767M undrawn revolver = $1.7B total liquidity. Net cash position of $309M (vs. $3.1B net debt in 2021). Product tanker rates already strong: LR2 near $46,000/day, MR near $38,000/day. Hormuz disruption would spike product tanker rates as refined fuel rerouting adds ton-miles. [Source: fool.com, tipranks.com, scorpiotankers.com]

- **DHT Holdings — Pure VLCC play with conservative valuation**: 22-VLCC fleet with 4 newbuilds delivering through mid-2026. Q4 2025: shipping revenue $143.9M, adjusted EBITDA $95.3M, net profit $66.1M ($0.41/share). VLCC spot earnings $69,500/day, combined TCE $60,300/day. Financial strength: current ratio 2.41, debt-to-equity 0.25. P/E 11.06x — cheap relative to rate environment. Fair value estimate ~$15.32 vs. recent trading ~$13. [Source: gurufocus.com, tipranks.com, seekingalpha.com]

- **INSW (International Seaways) — Diversified tanker with $1B+ returned to shareholders**: 70 vessels, 8.4M dwt. Q4 2025 net income $128M ($2.56/share). Full year: $843.3M shipping revenue, $474.7M adjusted EBITDA. Breakeven near $14,800/day. Q1 2026 spot TCE booked at ~$50,900/day (71% booked). Acquiring remaining 50% of Tankers International pool. Declared $2.15/share combined dividend for March 2026 (87% of adjusted net income). Has returned >$1B to shareholders since 2020. [Source: yahoo.com, stocktitan.com, aijourn.com]

- **NAT (Nordic American Tankers) — Suezmax pure play, dividend focus**: 20-24 Suezmax vessels (exclusively Suezmax, NOT VLCC). Q4 2025 TCE $35,000/day (up 25% QoQ), net income $11.7M, EBITDA $34.7M. 114th consecutive quarterly dividend at $0.17/share. Nearly 2/3 of Q1 2026 spot days booked at ~$55,000/day. Smaller scale and Suezmax-only focus makes it higher beta but less diversified than FRO or INSW. [Source: globenewswire.com, quiverquant.com, nat.bm]

### Red Sea Houthi Crisis Precedent (2023-2025)

- **Container traffic through Red Sea fell 90%** from December 2023 to February 2024. Suez Canal share of global maritime traffic fell from 12% to 9%. [Source: atlasinstitute.org, coface.com]

- **Freight rates surged sevenfold** between Nov 2023 and July 2024. Asia-Europe spot rates rose nearly 5x. Rates remained ~80% above pre-crisis levels through October 2025. [Source: coface.com, xeneta.com]

- **LR2 product tanker spot rates averaged $84,800/day** during the crisis, up 132% YoY, with ME-Gulf-Europe route averaging $92,100/day. [Source: freightwaves.com]

- **Key difference from Hormuz**: Red Sea/Suez handles ~12% of global container trade. Hormuz handles 20% of global oil consumption (~20-21M bpd) and 20% of global LNG. A Hormuz disruption is an order of magnitude more severe for energy markets, though the Red Sea precedent shows how quickly shipping rates respond to geopolitical chokepoint threats. [Source: theblueprintbrief.com, gulfnews.com]

### Container Shipping — Mixed Impact

- **Maersk and Hapag-Lloyd face overcapacity headwinds**: Maersk posted Q4 pre-tax loss with 2026 EBIT guidance ranging from -$1.5B to +$1.0B depending on Suez return speed. Average freight rates fell 16.2% QoQ to $1,310/TEU. Orderbook-to-fleet ratio at 31.6% signals structural oversupply. [Source: freightwaves.com, lloydslist.com, container-mag.com]

- **Iran war could temporarily reverse rate declines**: If Houthi attacks resume (in solidarity with Iran) and Gulf shipping lanes become unsafe, the rerouting via Cape of Good Hope that boosted rates in 2024 would intensify. However, this is a volatile trade — Houthi ceasefire in Oct 2025 already threatened to crash freight rates 25%+. [Source: xeneta.com, gulfnews.com]

- **ZIM acquisition by Hapag-Lloyd**: $4.2B deal signed, expected to close late 2026. ZIM's Israeli flag/association creates specific war risk — ZIM vessels were primary Houthi targets. Stock is effectively in M&A limbo. [Source: gcaptain.com, timesofisrael.com]

### Airlines — Short Candidates

- **Airspace closures are immediate and severe**: At least 8 states closed airspace on Feb 28 (Iran, Israel, Iraq, Jordan, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, UAE). Dubai International Airport (world's busiest international hub) suspended ALL flights. Emirates, Qatar Airways, and Etihad halted operations. [Source: thenationalnews.com, aljazeera.com, fortune.com]

- **Rerouting costs $6,000+/flight hour**: Flights between Europe and Southeast Asia must take significantly longer routes with Iranian/Iraqi airspace off-limits. Gulf carriers face structural increase in operating costs, weaker aircraft utilization, and profit margin pressure on long-haul networks reliant on Middle East transit corridors. [Source: thenationalnews.com, skift.com]

- **JETS ETF as short vehicle**: U.S. Global Jets ETF (JETS) tracks 49 airline and travel-related firms. Beta of 1.2 (vs 1.8 for individual airline stocks), providing diversified downside exposure. Top holdings: AAL, LUV, UAL, DAL. [Source: usglobaletfs.com, ainvest.com]

- **DAL, UAL, AAL fuel cost sensitivity**: Airlines typically hedge 30-60% of fuel expenses for next 6 months. Oil spike to $100-150/bbl would overwhelm hedging positions. Jet fuel represents ~25-35% of airline operating costs. Historical research shows that oil price shocks have "significantly adverse effects on real stock returns of airlines both at industry and at firm level." [Source: sciencedirect.com, pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov]

- **Gulf War 1990-91 airline precedent**: The 1990 oil spike ($17 to $40/bbl) contributed to 8-month recession. Airlines were among the hardest-hit sectors. S&P 500 fell ~20% during the recession period. The airline industry experienced multiple bankruptcies (Pan Am, Eastern, Midway) in 1990-91 with oil spike as a key catalyst. [Source: trendspider.com, wikipedia.org]

### Cruise Lines — Short Candidates

- **RCL, CCL, NCLH face fuel cost and demand double hit**: Higher oil prices directly increase bunker fuel costs. Royal Caribbean projected 2026 adjusted EPS of $17.70-18.10, which would be pressured by elevated fuel costs. Carnival has 12.4% earnings growth rate baked in for FY2026 that is at risk. [Source: fool.com, nasdaq.com]

- **Consumer confidence erosion**: War-driven consumer anxiety typically reduces discretionary travel spending. Cruise bookings are long-lead-time decisions — a war environment creates booking hesitation even for non-affected routes. Higher interest rates (if oil-driven inflation forces Fed tightening) compound the damage. [Source: financialcontent.com]

- **Itinerary disruptions**: Eastern Mediterranean and Middle East itineraries would be directly cancelled. Insurance costs for cruise operators transiting affected waters would spike. [Source: general analysis based on Red Sea precedent]

### Online Travel Agencies — Short Candidates

- **BKNG (Booking Holdings) and EXPE (Expedia) already under pressure**: Prior to the Feb 28 strikes, Booking Holdings stock was down 22.3% YTD and Expedia down 19.8% in 2026. The conflict adds Middle East booking cancellations, consumer confidence decline, and AI disruption fears (hotel chains inking direct AI booking deals). [Source: travelandtourworld.com, seekingalpha.com]

- **Middle East is a high-value travel corridor**: Dubai, Qatar, and other Gulf destinations are premium booking markets. Complete airspace closure directly eliminates this revenue stream. Cascading impact on connecting flights that route through Gulf hubs affects Asia-Europe bookings broadly. [Source: skift.com, thenationalnews.com]

### Emerging Markets — Short/Hedge Candidates

- **India (INDA ETF) — 85% oil import dependent**: India imports virtually all its crude oil, though structural reforms have reduced oil from ~37% of total imports (2014) to ~26% (2025). Every $10/bbl oil price increase widens India's current account deficit. FPIs view India as "high-beta" EM susceptible to global shocks — war-driven risk-off would trigger FPI outflows to safe havens. Aviation, paints, chemicals, and tire manufacturers most exposed to oil-based input cost pressure. [Source: outlookmoney.com, businesstoday.in, hdfcfund.com]

- **Turkey (TUR ETF) — Iran border and trade exposure**: Turkey shares a border with Iran and maintains deep economic ties. Broader conflict could destabilize Turkey's southeastern borders due to Iran-backed groups in Iraq/Syria. Turkish lira already fragile (lost 37% vs. USD in 2023). Oil-importing economy vulnerable to energy price spikes. Stock market correlated with oil prices and geopolitical risk. [Source: coface.com, state.gov, tandfonline.com]

- **EEM (iShares MSCI Emerging Markets ETF) — Broad EM oil sensitivity**: Capital flows show potential outflows from high-beta EMs into safe havens and energy exporters. Oil-importing EMs (India, Turkey, Thailand, Philippines, South Korea) face twin deficits from energy cost surge. VIX spike and risk-off sentiment compound EM equity pressure. [Source: investing.com, seekingalpha.com, cnbc.com]

- **Major oil companies suspending Hormuz shipments**: Several major oil companies and trading houses implemented formal suspension policies for oil/fuel shipments through the Strait of Hormuz following the Feb 28 strikes — an escalation from individual tanker avoidance indicating widespread industry concern. [Source: scanx.trade]
